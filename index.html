Hobbies

Hobby — PC Building

I don't just use computers.
I build them.

There's something deeply satisfying about choosing every component — the CPU, the cooler, the RAM timings — and knowing exactly why you made each call. My first build took a weekend and three YouTube tutorials. Now I can thermal-paste a CPU in my sleep.

The RGB is non-negotiable. Not because it makes the computer faster — it just makes the desk look incredible at 2 AM when I'm still debugging.

Hobby — Troubleshooting

Give me an error code.

Most people see an error and google the exact message. I see an error and start reading the stack trace — because the real problem is never where it first appears.

I've debugged everything from kernel panics to mysteriously silent API failures. Each one is a puzzle, and I genuinely can't leave a puzzle unsolved.

rathan@debug ~ bash

Hobby — PCB Explorer

Circuits are code in copper.

I started exploring PCBs when I realised software without hardware is only half the story. Tracing signal paths on a board is like reading assembly — tedious until suddenly it all makes perfect sense.

I'm not an electrical engineer, but I understand enough to read schematics, test with a multimeter, and know exactly when a capacitor is the wrong value.

Hobby — PlayStation

The best stories I know came from a controller.

PlayStation taught me about narrative design before I knew that was a thing. Playing The Last of Us at 14 felt like reading the most emotionally demanding book I'd ever encountered — except I was the one making the choices.

I'm a completionist at heart. If there's a trophy for finding every collectible in a 60-hour open world, I will find every single one. This is not healthy. I regret nothing.

Hobby — Origami

One sheet of paper. Infinite forms.

Origami is where I go when I need to think without a screen. There's something meditative about 48 deliberate folds turning into a dragon — no undo, no ctrl+z, just paper and patience.

It also turns out that spatial reasoning for origami and system architecture aren't that different. Both are about understanding structure before you commit to a fold you can't reverse.

Hobby — 3D Artist

Every mesh starts with one vertex.

I got into 3D art through Blender — originally to make assets for a game idea that never launched. The idea died; the obsession with topology and lighting didn't.

There's a parallel between good 3D modeling and good software architecture. In both: the cleaner the underlying structure, the better everything built on top of it looks.

Hobby — 2D Illustration

I draw to think differently.

Before I open a code editor, I sketch on paper. Architecture diagrams, UI flows, even abstract doodles of how I imagine data moving through a system. Drawing engages a different part of my brain — and the ideas that come from it are usually the best ones.

I do character illustrations, landscapes, and the occasional surrealist piece where a circuit board becomes a city. My medium of choice is Procreate — but there's still something about a sketchbook and a ballpoint pen.

Hobby — Skating

Fail. Land. Repeat.

Skating taught me that failure is just information. You bail a trick, you analyse what went wrong — foot placement, timing, weight — and you try again. It's debugging, but for your body.

The skateboard doesn't care about your resume. It's the most honest feedback loop I've ever found. Land it or you don't. No participation trophies on asphalt.
